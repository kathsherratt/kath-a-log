# EMS 2026 Abstract: Multi-model ensembles in epidemic forecasting

## Title
Do multi-model ensembles improve epidemic forecasting? A systematic review

## Abstract

Epidemic forecasting has shifted from individual models to multi-model ensembles. During COVID-19, forecasting hubs aggregated dozens of models into ensemble projections for policy. Does more modelling improve epidemic intelligence?

We conducted a systematic review assessing the predictive accuracy of multi-model ensemble forecasts of infectious disease, and the benefits and challenges of such collaborations. We searched MEDLINE, Embase, medRxiv, bioRxiv, and arXiv for empirical studies comparing ensemble projections to component models. We extracted data on ensemble performance (primary outcome), and process and impact factors (secondary outcomes).

Ensembles tend to outperform individual models, but gains are modest and context-dependent. Performance improves with short horizons and early epidemic phase; degradation is sharper in late phase and novel scenarios. However, model independence is rarely characterised: many "independent" models share data, code, or assumptions. The "forecast combination puzzle"—why simple averaging matches sophisticated weighting—holds in infectious disease.

Secondary outcomes reveal a substantial gap. Process factors (onboarding, consensus time, resources) and impact factors (policy relevance, communication, stakeholder acceptance) are rarely reported. We scaled forecasting infrastructure with almost no evaluation of whether it supports decision-making.

This raises two challenges for statisticians: the information structure of epidemic forecasts is poorly understood—what makes models independent? Why does simplicity prevail?—and predictive accuracy is not equivalent to usefulness. Ensembles performing well on standard metrics may not provide actionable evidence. The field needs evaluation frameworks that measure decision-relevance alongside accuracy.
